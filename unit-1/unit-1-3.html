1.3 Grammar. A degree of modification

We begin with grammar because, as the backbone of language, the teacher must be not only fully conversant but also fully confident in this area. You need to be able to teach it to your students and be able to deal with their questions. Fortunately, we have come a long way from the Grammar Translation Method and are now using communicative teaching in our daily practice, but we cannot ignore that grammar rules exist and knowing them is your job. Furthermore, learners have different learning styles, and many both require and demand a good grammatical “skeleton” if they are to master a language. So as to develop a comprehensive knowledge of English grammar, a good reference book like Swan (2005), referred to in unit one, will be your chief ally. If you do not already own one, I recommend you consider buying one as an 'investment purchase' as it is a tool you will use for the rest of your teaching career.

A degree of modification

This first grammar topic encourages you to explore the uses and meanings of modifying adverbs, as well as so and such.

To reinforce your understanding, look at these “cold” sentences.

  • It’s very cold indeed.
  • It’s rather cold today.
  • It’s somewhat cold today (all of these are relatively formal).
  • It’s a bit cold, so I’m not going out.
  • It’s a bit cold, but I’m going out anyway (“a bit” is very informal).
  • It’s a little cold, so I’m not going out.
  • It’s a little cold, but I’m going out anyway (“a little” is relatively formal).
  • It’s cold enough to wear my new sweater.
  • It isn’t cold enough to wear my new sweater (or, it’s not cold enough).
  • It’s cold enough to wear my new sweater.
  • It isn’t cold enough to wear my new sweater (or, it’s not cold enough).
  • It’s too cold, so I’m not going out.
  • It isn’t cold at all, so I’m going out (or, it’s not cold at all).
  • It isn’t very cold, so I’m going out (or, it’s not very cold).

Note that although essentially neutral, “a bit” and “a little” are used here with a negative connotation. However, they are never used with a directly positive adjective. You can say, “it’s a bit nasty” but not “it’s a bit nice,” or “it’s a bit obvious,” but not “it’s a bit straightforward”. “Nasty” is negative, “obvious” is neutral, but “nice” and “straightforward” are positive and will need stronger modification.
