1.7 Grammar. Gerund or infinitive?

As with prepositions the grammar constructions featuring gerunds and infinitives need to be learned rather than deduced.

Example:

  • I like doing...
  • I would like to do...
  • I enjoy doing...
  • I need to do...

Those are easy, but try to think of as many examples as you can, check in with your peers.

Go to this site for more information: Gerunds and Infinitives.

A little extra - Idioms, slang, colloquial expressions and all that jazz...

Not technically grammar, but...

These are interesting but tricky. Some of the more common ones turn up in listening tests, mostly to distract you, but their main use is in informal conversation with native speakers. Since language, especially informal, colloquial, “street” language, is constantly evolving, so too are these expressions. Don’t knock yourself out (?) trying to learn them, because, as in every language, you really have to understand how they work and if you try and force them into a conversation you can sound very strange indeed.

Also, some are still in use even though they’re as old as the hills (?) while others are out of date (like that one) and some get dropped before you’ve really cottoned on (?) to them. Probably the worst thing you can do is try and translate them because these are cultural concepts that rarely travel successfully. My all-time favorite comes from the north of England: “As daft as a brush.” I know what it means but it makes no sense in English or Spanish. Tonto como un cepillo, anyone?
